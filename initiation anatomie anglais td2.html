I. Anatomical description Arthrology models: Hip, knee.



1. General overview of a joint: Arthrology


Using the hip joint model, the following can be identified:
- The upper end of the femur: the femoral epiphysis
- The hip bone (coxal bone): the head of the femur fits into a cavity
- The hip joint capsule attaches to both bones, sealing the joint. The cavity is filled with fluid that allows the articular surfaces to glide against one another. This fluid is called synovial fluid.
- The capsule is reinforced by ligaments in areas subject to high stress. In such cases, the ligament is extra-articular (examples: iliofemoral ligaments, glenohumeral ligaments). Ligaments can also be intra-articular (examples: the ligament of the head of the femur [round ligament] in the hip joint, the cruciate ligaments in the knee joint).

2. Example: The hip joint (coxofemoral joint)


Joints are generally named based on the bones that compose them. The proximal bone (in this case, the hip bone or *os coxae*) is named first, followed by the distal bone (the femur), resulting in the name "coxofemoral joint."

"Proximal" means closest to the body's axis (represented by the vertebral column).


Description of the proximal end of the femur:
Composed of three parts: the head of the femur (or femoral head), the neck, and the trochanters (lesser and greater).
Description of the socket: shaped like a cup-like hollow, or acetabulum.
Description of the capsule: a fibrous sleeve attaching to the bones that form the joint.
Description of the ligaments: note their locations and orientations.
Type of joint: Ball-and-socket joint (*énarthrose*): this type of joint allows movement in all three planes of space.
Note:
Joints are highly mobile when movement is possible in all three planes of space.
- Examples:
o Hip joint (coxofemoral joint)
o Shoulder joint (glenohumeral joint)
Joints may also be mobile in two planes of space.

- Examples:
o Knee joint: flexion/extension and medial rotation/lateral rotation.
o Metacarpophalangeal joint: Flexion/extension and ABD/ADD

Joints may allow movement in only a single spatial plane.
- Examples:
o Proximal interphalangeal joint
o Distal interphalangeal joint.

  II. Planes, axes, and movement

Example of the hip: Review of concepts covered in Tutorial 1
• Flexion / Extension:
Hip flexion is a movement occurring in a sagittal plane around a transverse axis passing through the hip joint (coxofemoral joint), bringing the lower limb anterior to the frontal plane.
Hip extension is a movement occurring in a sagittal plane around a transverse axis passing through the hip joint (coxofemoral joint), bringing the lower limb posterior to the frontal plane.
• Abduction / Adduction:
To avoid confusion, the terms should be pronounced as "ABD" and "ADD," articulating each letter.

Hip abduction (or ABD) is a movement occurring in a frontal plane around a sagittal axis passing through the hip joint (coxofemoral joint), moving the medial aspect of the lower limb away from the body's midline.
Hip adduction (or ADD) is a movement occurring in a frontal plane around a sagittal axis passing through the hip joint (coxofemoral joint), moving the medial aspect of the lower limb toward the body's midline.
Note: This adduction movement can only be performed if the contralateral lower limb is moved out of the way.

• Lateral rotation / Medial rotation:
Lateral rotation (or external rotation) of the hip is a movement occurring in the transverse plane around a craniocaudal axis passing through the hip joint; it turns the anterior aspect of the thigh outward (symbolized as DH).
Medial rotation (or internal rotation) of the hip is a movement occurring in the transverse plane around a craniocaudal axis passing through the hip joint; it turns the anterior aspect of the thigh inward (symbolized as DD).

Practical exercises.
Vocabulary tailored to the movement. Instructions must be simple and precise.
Flexion / Extension: Bend, straighten, lift, lower…
ABD/ADD: Move away, bring closer…
Rotation: "Push against my hand," move away, bring closer…
III. Learning methodology:
Regular, continuous practice: You learn seven times and forget six!
Hold the bones in your hands to refine your palpation skills and memorize the terminology.
